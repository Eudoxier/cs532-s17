5.0 out of 5 starsA Mirror of Many of My Techie Experiences.
ByK. VarrasoVINE VOICEon April 19, 2016
Format: Hardcover|Vine Customer Review of Free Product( What's this? )
I worked in computer technology almost (cough) 35 years ago, after falling into it by accident, and loving the opportunities it provided. Like the author, I was rather surprised that none of the other women around me could recognize what a great deal working as a techie could be. I thought that perhaps the good deal available in tech work had probably changed, but the author makes the case that there is still plenty of room for women in tech, maybe even more than when I was working.

She has several female tech workers describe their experiences, which had me nodding in agreement, remembering experiences I had years ago, which should have disappeared ages ago. That part is pathetic. However, the rest of the book is wonderful, describing what life is like for a female techie in a male world. She also goes through the whole process of preparing a resume, going through the resume process, negotiating a decent salary (worth the price of the book all by itself!) and living in a man's and computer's world.

All I can say is that this did a lot of mirroring my own experiences, with just the dates on the calendar changed. I'm sorry that zero, or less than zero progress has been made, but really glad that the field is wide open for young women heading into the field. There's also a lot of information for women who want to improve their lives by moving into tech. If you like solving problems and want to move into a rapidly expanding field, read this. It could change your life.

4.0 out of 5 starsHelpful starter guide for young female technology professionals (mainly programmers)
ByEugeniaon April 27, 2016
Format: Hardcover|Vine Customer Review of Free Product( What's this? )
As a woman who works in technology I was very interested in reading this book. Author has a number of female contributors (one of them is transgender, which I found to be very interesting) who wrote articles about how they became involved in technology during their professional careers. I found it to be very interesting that many of the women had non-traditional path to working in technology. They were self-thought programmers, some were college dropouts and many received education in non-technical field but ended up working as programmers.

I found book to be empowering and inspirational for all women of all ages who are paving their way in this field. There are many helpful tips on how to write technical resume (although I wish samples of the resumes are included in the book, which they are not; author is encouraging reader to search her blogs and web sites for the sample of them), how to network, how to start one's own company and how to brand oneself in the industry largely populated by male counterparts.

Author also mentions two of the best known female CEOs in technology: Marisa Meyer and Sheryl Sandberg. Although Ms. Wheeler Van Vlack is not openly expressing her opinions about either one of them as professionals, I can say that I find neither of them to be worthy female role models for women in technology today. Ms. Meyer is quite unpopular at Yahoo and many technical writers are writing about her (mis)treatment of Yahoo employees and her chronic disregard of other people's (CEOs who are unfortunate to have business relationship with her) time. As far as Ms. Sandberg is concerned, she is really a woman who studied business and leads technology company, but she herself is not an engineer. I particularly like Ms. Barecca's criticism of Ms. Sandberg. Ms. Gina Barecca's latest book has a satiric story of Ms. Sandberg's best-selling book "Lean In" - that has appropriately modified the title in Ms. Barecca's short story is "If I Lean In, Will Men Look Down My Shirt?" that Is also a title of her latest book (you can buy it on Amazon). It is a must read for every woman, that will explain how clueless privileged women (like Ms. Sandberg) are about the daily lives of ordinary working women.

I also have to mention that I started my career as engineer and I worked for companies such as Lucent and Avaya. Over time, I transitioned into project management role and I find it disturbing that author, along with some of the contributors in this book, does not emphasize that many women can have lucrative, successful and fulfilling careers in project management. On the contrary, many of contributors in this book have unfavorable view of the role of the technology project managers (male of female). Book even refers to the project managers as "para-technical" (?!).

It is a quick read, very informative, but I cannot say I agree with everything that is said in the book. There was a time in my life when I was multitasking various roles in my life in a same manner that Ms. Wheeler Van Vlack is describing her time management practices today, especially towards the end of the book. I can say I consider myself successful. I love my career as a project manager and I also consider myself to be a "servant leader". In my spare time, I teach yoga and educate people on importance of honoring nature, people around us, finding time to relax, exercise and meditate and find healthy balance between professional and personal life. I no longer attend networking events because I find them increasingly annoying. Many people (male and female) are treating such events as a way to ask for jobs and to me personally, networking is about creating new relationships, not about self-promotion and job search. For a while I was mentoring young college graduates and I find that new generation of college students are more interested in finding contacts in a company where they are seeking jobs, then looking for a mentor that can teach them what working life is all about and how it is different form being in school. Not to mention that their lack of manners (saying thank you, sending a thank you note, or even staying in touch with their mentors) is non-existent). And yes, manners do matter to me. I personally consider it that if people do not have basic manners, they certainly should not consider themselves "professional" no matter what type of education or level of degree they have. I also love to read on variety of topic. I find it to be helpful in relating to all kinds of people of all backgrounds and ages.
